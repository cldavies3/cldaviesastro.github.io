Observing

Center for High Angular Resolution Astronomy (CHARA)

I am part of a team of astronomers from the Universities of Exeter (UK) and Michigan (USA) working on the commissioning of a new infra-red (J- and H-band) detector for the Center for High Angular Resolution Astronomy (CHARA) Array. This detector, called MIRCx, serves as an upgrade to the previous infra-red detector for the six telescope beam combiner, MIRC.

My role in the MIRCx team concerns observations, data reduction, and documentation. During the combined commissioning and observing run of July 2017, I spent 16 nights at the CHARA Array. During this time, we successfully installed the MIRCx camera and obtained our first interferometric fringes using the six telescope beam combiner. We observed a handful of spectroscopic binary stars which are helping us perform quality checks on the data.

These were supplemented with observations of young stellar objects: stars still enshrouded in gas and dust left over from their collapse from molecular clouds. I, and other members of the MIRCx team will use these observations to study the formation of stars and the evolution of dust grains in their circumstellar disks.

I returned to the observatory in November 2017 and May 2018 during which time I successfully observed a handful of post-AGB binary stars, massive binaries, Cepheid variables, Be stars and young stellar objects. We hope to use the uv-plane coverage provided by the simultaneous use of all six CHARA telescopes to reconstruct images of thse stars and/or their circumstellar environments.

In October 2016, prior to the MIRCx upgrade, I travelled to California to use its predecesor, MIRC at the CHARA Array. The observing run was comprised of 10 nights on-sky during which time we focussed our attentions on following the orbit of an exoplanet (a planet that orbits a star other than the Sun) known to be several times the mass of Jupiter while orbiting closer to its host star than Mercury does to our Sun. These types of planets are known as hot Jupiters. The data is presently being analysed by a PhD student at the University of Michigan.


Very Large Telescope Interferometer

I have successfully proposed observations using the GRAVITY instrument on the Very Large Telescope Interferometer (VLTI) in the mountains of Paranal, Chile in each of its operating semesters. GRAVITY combines the light (K-band) from the four auxiliary telescopes or unit telescopes of the VLTI.


James Clark Maxwell Telescope

In February/March 2013, May 2014 and September 2014, I travelled to Hawai'i to observe debris discs using the SCUBA-2 instrument on the James Clark Maxwell Telescope (JCMT) as part of the SONS JCMT Legacy Survey (11 nights total). The telescope is situated on the mountain of Mauna Kea at a height of over 13,000 feet above sea level. At this elevation, the amount of water vapour in the atmosphere is greatly reduced, allowing astronomers to achieve better quality observations.

With the JCMT, we were searching for the presence of thermal emission from dust around nearby stars. This dust exists in large ring-like structures akin to the asteroid belt or Kuiper belt in our own Solar System. These rings are known as debris disks and, as the name suggests, are understood to consist of the dusty debris from the collisions of comets and planetary-sized bodies. This work is now published and can be accessed for free here.


I am indebted to the European Research Council and the Science and Technology Facilities Council for their provision of funds to enable me to visit and conduct my research at these observatories. I also recognise and appreciate the very significant cultural role that the summit of Maunakea (on which the James Clark Maxwell Telescope sits) has. I acknowledge that I have been extremely fortunate to have the opportunity to conduct observations from this mountain.
