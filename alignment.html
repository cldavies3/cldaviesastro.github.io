Star-disk misalignment

As an increasing number of planets are discovered around stars other than our Sun, we are finding more and more exotic types of exoplanets. One of these species of exoplanets are the hot-Jupiters: planets of roughly a Jupiter mass or larger which have orbital periods less than about 10 days. In comparison, the closest planet to the Sun in the Solar System is Mercury which takes 88 days to complete one orbit. Observations of the Rossiter-McLaughlin effect among transiting exoplanets has revealed that around 40% of hot-Jupiters exist on orbits that are inclined with respect to their stellar hosts' rotation axis.

There are three broad groupings of theories to explain how this can happen: (i) the rotation axis of the host star changes during its evolution towards the main sequence; (ii) the orbital axis of the exoplanet changes during its formation and evolution or; (iii) the rotation axis of the disk out of which the planet forms changes. I use high angular observations of disks to investigate the occurence rate of option (iii) and search for primordial misalignments between stars and their disks.

Recent results:

Earlier this year, I looked at a sample of twenty stars with protoplanetary disks in two star forming regions (Rho Ophiuchus and Upper Scorpius) for which a estimate of the stellar and the disk inclination could be estimated. I found that 36% of stars in the sample appeared misaligned with their outer disks. I constructed a simulated dataset to investigate whether a sample of aligned systems could artificially replicate this result due to measurement uncertainties and biases. I found that while the 100% aligned simulated dataset could replicate the frequency of misalignment, it could not replicate the distribution of misalignment angles observed. More details can be found in the paper which can be accessed here.
