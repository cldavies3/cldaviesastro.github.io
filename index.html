About me

I am currently a European Research Council funded Research Fellow at the University of Exeter, UK, in the research group of Prof. Stefan Kraus.

My present research is focussed on the analysis of near-infrared interferometric observations of protoplanetary disks using worldwide astronomical facilities such as the Center for High Angular Resolution Astronomy (CHARA) and the Very Large Telescope Interferometer (VLTI). To analyse these observations, I use and develop the Monte Carlo radiative transfer code, TORUS, originally developed by Prof. Tim Harries. I also use and develop the Exeter geometrical modelling pipeline, RAPIDO (Radiative transfer and Analytic modelling Pipeline for Interferometric Disk Observations), to model continuum and line emission from disks. These analyses probe the innermost regions of proto-planetary disks, allowing us to study their geometry, composition and shape. From this, we can learn more about planet formation and disk accretion processes. Further details regarding my scientific research can be found here and further information regarding my observing experience is provided here.

Prior to arriving in Exeter in October 2015, I worked at the University of St Andrews, UK, as an STFC STEP Fellow following the completion of my PhD at the same institution in April 2015. My PhD thesis, entitled "Revolution evolution: tracing angular momentum during star and planetary system formation" focussed on observationally probing the efficiency of stellar spin down during gravitational collapse together with quantifying the circumstellar angular momentum retained within planetary systems at various stages of formation. A copy of my thesis can be downloaded following links provided in my publications section.

Aside from my scientific research, I am also involved in various public outreach activites. Details of these can be found in the outreach section. I am a member of the Institute of Physics and a Fellow of the Royal Astronomical Society. I have recently been co-opted onto the IOP South West branch committee with the aim of delivering physics related events to communities in the South West of England. I also sit on the University of Exeter's Physics and Astronomy Inclusivity Working Group which is working towards creating a more inclusive work environment celebrating the diversity of all staff and students. I am also a member of the University of Exeter's LGBTQ+ staff coordinators group.

Recent News

July 5th 2018

Launch of PRISM Exeter

In conjunction with the first International day for LGBTQ+ people in STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering & Mathematics), I, with assistance from employees of the Met Office, Exeter Science Park, and fellow members of the College of Engineering, Mathematics and Physical Sciences at the University of Exeter, have launched an Exeter-wide network for LGBTQ+ people working in STEM fields. The network will be known as PRISM Exeter and more information will follow in the coming weeks.

June 22nd 2018

Talk awarded at ESO's Take a Closer Look Workshop

I received news that the talk abstract I submitted for ESO's Take A Closer Look Workshop due to take place at ESO Garching in October has been accepted. In the talk, I will discuss the work I have been doing modelling CHARA/CLIMB observations of a young stellar object (HD 142666) with the TORUS radiative transfer code.

In conjunction with this, I also successfully managed to acquire funds to cover the conference attendance fees of three post-docs (including myself) and a PhD student from our interferometry group in Exeter as well as the accomodation fees of the student. This has facilitated the attendance of a large portion of our group to attend and present our work at this workshop.

June 11th - 22nd 2018

CHARA Observing run with MIRCx

I am back on Mount Wilson for the second month in a row to conduct observations of pre-main sequence binaries (CHARA proposal 2018A:M4) and young stellar objects (proposal 2018A:M3).

May 14th - 25th 2018

CHARA Observing run with MIRCx

As part of my role within the MIRCx collaboration, I visited CHARA to conduct observations of young stellar objects during the ongoing installation of adaptive optics systems (CHARA proposal 2018A:M5), the engineering of our new camera for the six-telescope beam combiner, MIRCx (CHARA proposal 2018A:M2) and for the 2018A:M4 program to study pre-main-sequence star binary systems. On UT date May 24th, with the assistance of Narsireddy Anugu, Aaron Labdon and Alex Kreplin, we successfully deomstrated the remote observing capabilities of MIRCx from the University of Exeter directly for the first time. This is a fantastic step forward in making UK-based MIRCx observations more economical and provides us with a training utility for future UK-based CHARA Array users.

May 12th 2018

Institute of Physics south west branch stall at Exeter Pride

For the 10th anniversary of Exeter Pride - the local LGBTQ+ festival (and largest in Devon) - I organised for the South West branch of the Institute of Physics to have a stall in the "marketplace" area. This was the first time a science-themed stall or event had featured at the festival. The stall featured hands-on demonstrations of physics and astronomy, information for STEM-employees in and around Exeter regarding the newly-launched LGBTQ+ Physical Sciences Network, and promoted the work of individuals within the LGBTQ+ STEM community. The success of this event was helped by involvement from the Institute of Physics, the Royal Astronomical Society, Pride in STEM and volunteers from the University of Exeter, the Met Office, and Network Rail.

Apr 13th 2018

Public outreach talk for Plymouth Astronomical Society

I returned for the second time to Plymouth Astronomical Society, this time to present a talk on "Star and Planetary System Formation". The talk outlined how stars form, how planets form, and what else constitutes a planetary system. I spoke at the end about some of the unanswered questions regarding the formation of high-obliquity hot Jupiters and how I intend to begin to answer these questions using optical interferometry. The talk, attended by ~50 people, was well received with lots of interesting questions.
