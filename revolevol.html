Revolution Evolution

If stars conserved all their angular momentum during their gravitational collapse from molecular cloud cores their rotational velocities would be large enough to tear the stars apart. This problem is exacerbated during the accretion process, requiring stars to transfer away a large portion of their angular momentum to avoid reaching break-up velocities.

Thesis work:

This research formed a large portion of my PhD thesis. Using a sample of fully convective stars in the Orion Nebula Cluster and the Taurus-Auriga star forming regions, I found the stellar angular momentum removal rate exceeded that predicted by simple disk-locking theories whereby stars contract at fixed rotation rates. Moreover, I found tentative evidence that the disparity between the rotation period distributions of fully convective stars more massive than about 0.2 solar masses and their lower mass counterparts appears due (at least in part) to differences magnetic field geometry differences.

Present/future work:

I am involved in projects looking further into the relationship between stellar rotation rates during contraction towards the main sequence and the stellar interior which impacts on the surface magnetic field strength and topology. At present, the number of young stellar objects with mapped magnetic fields is small. I am part of the Magnetic Protostars scientific working group, one of the SPiRou Legacy Surveys which study the magnetic fields of young stars across the pre-main-sequence HR diagram.
